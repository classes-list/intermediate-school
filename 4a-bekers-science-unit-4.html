🏔️ Shaping Landforms

Essential Question: How does Earth's surface change over time, and what evidence do we have?

📖 Unit Storyline

Students explore how landscapes change over time. They use models to examine the factors and processes that transform land, then construct explanations from patterns in their observations.

LESSON 1 Factors That Shape Earth's Surface
NGSS: 4-ESS2-1

🎯 Objective

Plan and carry out investigations to make observations of the effects of water, ice, wind, and vegetation on landforms.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A canyon formed in the middle of flat land. What could have made it?

🧪 Activities

  • Anchoring Phenomenon: Disappearing Beach — students observe how a beach changes over months.
  • Investigation: Modeling How Far Sediment Travels — students pour water down a tray of sand at different slopes and measure how far sand moves.
  • Hands-On: A Sweet Test — students use sugar cubes and shaking to model rock weathering.
  • Comparison: Earth's Surface — students compare pictures of the same location over decades.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Weathering breaks rocks into smaller pieces.
  • Erosion moves those pieces (sediment) to new places via water, wind, ice, or gravity.
  • Deposition lays sediment down in a new location, sometimes building landforms like deltas.
  • Living things — roots, burrowing animals — also shape the land.

LESSON 2 Fast and Slow Changes
NGSS: 4-ESS2-1

🎯 Objective

Compare fast and slow processes that change Earth's surface.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A rockslide can happen in seconds — but the mountain that produced it took millions of years to form.

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: The Rate of Change — students categorize processes (rockfall, glacier, earthquake, canyon carving) as fast or slow.
  • Modeling: Glaciers on the Move — students use ice cubes and sand to model glacier scraping.
  • Video/Discussion: Fast Changes — students watch videos of volcanoes, landslides, and floods.
  • Compare: Slow Changes — students compare glaciers and rivers to see how slow processes still shape huge features.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Fast changes include earthquakes, landslides, volcanoes, and floods.
  • Slow changes include glaciers moving, rivers carving valleys, wind eroding rocks.
  • Both kinds of change can create dramatic landforms.
  • By observing patterns, we can predict what changes might happen in a place.

LESSON 3 Rock Layers Record Landform Changes
NGSS: 4-ESS1-1

🎯 Objective

Identify evidence from patterns in rock formations and fossils in rock layers to support an explanation for changes in a landscape over time.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A cliff shows many horizontal layers of rock, each a different color. What does each layer tell us?

🧪 Activities

  • Modeling: Layered Landforms — students build layered rock models using different-colored clay.
  • Hands-On: Layer by Layer — students press "fossils" (small objects) into modeling clay layers.
  • Investigation: Layers of Rock — students order rock strata by age based on which is on top.
  • Discussion: Evidence of Environments — students look at fossil pictures and infer what the environment was like when the fossil was buried.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Older rock layers are usually on the BOTTOM; newer layers are on TOP.
  • Fossils in rock layers show what plants and animals lived there in the past.
  • A layer of ocean fossils in the desert is evidence that the desert was once underwater.
  • By studying rock layers, geologists can piece together Earth's history.
