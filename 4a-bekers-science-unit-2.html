🌱 Plant and Animal Structure and Function

Essential Question: How do the internal and external structures of plants and animals help them survive?

📖 Unit Storyline

Students explore how plant and animal structures support survival, growth, and reproduction. They develop and use models, then argue from evidence to explain how organisms' senses help them survive.

LESSON 1 Plant Structures — Roots, Stems, Leaves
NGSS: 4-LS1-1

🎯 Objective

Investigate how the non-reproductive structures of plants (roots, stems, leaves) support survival and growth.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Why does an apple tree look different in each season, and how do its structures keep it alive?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Root Race — students grow two bean plants, one in soil and one in a rocky cup, and compare root growth.
  • Hands-On: Celery Straw — students place celery in colored water to observe how a stem carries water up to the leaves.
  • Leaf Rubbing & Analysis — students collect leaves, do a rubbing, and identify the vein pattern for water transport.
  • Modeling: Draw a whole plant and label each structure with its function.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Roots anchor the plant and absorb water and nutrients from soil.
  • Stems transport water and nutrients between roots and leaves, and support the plant.
  • Leaves collect sunlight and make food through photosynthesis.

LESSON 2 Plant Reproduction — Flowers, Seeds & Pollination
NGSS: 4-LS1-1

🎯 Objective

Explore how flowers, fruits, and seeds work together to produce new plants.

Anchoring Phenomenon: How can a single apple make so many new apple trees?

🧪 Activities

  • Hands-On: Flower Power — students dissect a flower and identify petals, stamen (male), and pistil (female).
  • Investigation: Seed Search — students open different fruits (apple, pepper, tomato) and count/compare seeds.
  • Modeling: Pollinator Match-Up — students match pollinators (bees, hummingbirds, wind, bats) with flower features that attract them.
  • Seed Sprout Lab — plant bean seeds in a plastic bag and observe germination for a week.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Flowers are the reproductive structures of most plants.
  • Pollination is when pollen from the stamen reaches the pistil — often carried by bees, wind, or birds.
  • Fruits protect seeds and help them travel to new places.
  • A seed contains everything needed to grow a new plant.

LESSON 3 Animal External Structures & Adaptations
NGSS: 4-LS1-1

🎯 Objective

Compare external structures (fur, feathers, scales, camouflage) and how they help animals survive in their environments.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Why do polar bears have white fur while grizzly bears have brown?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Camouflage Hunt — students "hunt" for hidden colored paper in different backgrounds and record how many they missed.
  • Feather Test — students spray water on real feathers to see waterproofing.
  • Habitat Match — students match animal features (webbed feet, thick fur, gills) to appropriate habitats.
  • Modeling: Draw an animal well-adapted to Hawaiʻi's beaches — label three external structures and explain how each helps.

💡 Key Ideas

  • External structures include fur, feathers, scales, shells, and skin coverings.
  • Camouflage helps an animal blend in and avoid predators.
  • External structures often match the environment (thick fur in cold places, scales in dry ones).
  • An adaptation is a body feature that helps an animal survive.

LESSON 4 Animal Internal Structures & Behaviors
NGSS: 4-LS1-1

🎯 Objective

Explore internal structures (skeleton, muscles, organs) and behaviors that support growth, survival, and reproduction.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A sea star can survive being torn in half. What internal structures make that possible?

🧪 Activities

  • Diagram: Inside Out — students label the digestive system of a common animal.
  • Compare Skeletons — students compare skeleton diagrams of humans, cats, and fish and notice patterns.
  • Investigation: Dinner Is Served — students match animal mouth/teeth structures to the foods they eat.
  • Modeling: Courtship Displays — students research how peacocks, tree frogs, and songbirds use behaviors and structures for reproduction.

💡 Key Ideas

  • The skeleton gives shape, supports the body, and protects internal organs.
  • The heart and blood vessels move oxygen and nutrients throughout the body.
  • The digestive system breaks down food for energy and building blocks.
  • Behaviors like courtship displays and care of young also help animals reproduce.

LESSON 5 Senses, Signals & Response
NGSS: 4-LS1-2

🎯 Objective

Use a model to describe how animals sense the environment, process the information, and respond.

Anchoring Phenomenon: How does an owl find a mouse in complete darkness?

🧪 Activities

  • Hands-On: Touch Test — students test which parts of the body have the most sensitive touch receptors.
  • Investigation: No Smell, No Taste, No See — students hold their nose while tasting jelly beans and describe what changes.
  • Modeling: Nerves and Receptors — students draw the pathway from stimulus → sensory receptor → brain → response.
  • Compare: Sights and Sounds — students compare animal senses (bat echolocation, dog smell, eagle vision).

💡 Key Ideas

  • Sensory receptors take in information from the environment (light, sound, touch, smell, taste).
  • The brain processes that information and decides what to do.
  • The body responds — usually by moving or changing behavior.
  • Different animals have different sensory strengths that fit where they live.
