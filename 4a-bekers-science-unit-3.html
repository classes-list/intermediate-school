⚡ Energy and Communication

Essential Question: How is energy transferred, and how do we use it to send information?

📖 Unit Storyline

Students explore how waves show that energy is transferred and how energy and information travel through waves. They investigate devices — from cell phones to satellites — that enable humans to transmit information across vast distances.

LESSON 1 Energy Transfer and Transformation
NGSS: 4-PS3-2, 4-PS3-4

🎯 Objective

Investigate how energy is transferred and transformed among objects.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Band members cause different kinds of energy to be transferred and transformed as they play and sing.

🧪 Activities

  • Hands-On: Full of Energy — students identify examples of energy at home and in the classroom.
  • Experiment: Light the Bulb — students build a simple circuit with a battery and bulb to see electrical → light and heat energy.
  • Investigation: Energy Is All Around — students test how heat energy transfers between warm and cool water.
  • Model: Energy of Sound — students strike a tuning fork and place it in water to see vibration → wave energy.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Energy can be transferred from one object to another (kicking a ball) or transformed from one form to another (electrical → light).
  • Forms of energy include: motion, heat, light, sound, and electrical.
  • Energy is never lost — it just changes form.
  • The faster an object moves, the more energy it has.

LESSON 2 Collisions
NGSS: 4-PS3-1, 4-PS3-3

🎯 Objective

Use evidence to explain how energy transfers during a collision.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A wrecking ball uses stored energy to build momentum and collide with a wall.

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Test It! Stored Energy in a Rubber Band — students stretch a rubber band different amounts and measure how far it launches an object.
  • Hands-On: Speed and Energy — students roll marbles at different speeds and observe collisions.
  • Model: Things That Move Have Energy — students diagram the energy transfer when a bowling ball hits pins.
  • Data Analysis: Collisions — students record and graph how initial speed affects how far the target moves.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Stored (potential) energy converts to motion (kinetic) energy.
  • The faster something is moving, the more energy it transfers in a collision.
  • Energy transfers between the colliding objects but is not destroyed.
  • Bigger objects also transfer more energy at the same speed.

LESSON 3 Waves
NGSS: 4-PS4-1

🎯 Objective

Develop a model to describe waves and how they transfer energy.

Anchoring Phenomenon: A wave in the ocean travels far, but the water itself mostly bobs in place.

🧪 Activities

  • Hands-On: Let's Make Waves! — students shake a rope to create transverse waves and observe amplitude.
  • Investigation: Bobbing and Waving — students place a rubber duck in water and watch how it bobs when waves pass.
  • Model: How Waves Transfer Energy — students draw the parts of a wave (crest, trough, amplitude, wavelength).
  • Compare: Wave Parts — students label the same features on sound and water waves.

💡 Key Ideas

  • A wave is a pattern of motion that transfers energy without moving the material long distances.
  • The amplitude is the height of the wave — bigger amplitude = more energy.
  • The wavelength is the distance between two crests.
  • Waves come in many forms: water, sound, light, and even earthquakes.

LESSON 4 Information Transfer
NGSS: 4-PS4-3, 3-5-ETS1-1

🎯 Objective

Generate and compare solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

Anchoring Phenomenon: How can text and pictures on a phone travel across the ocean in less than a second?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Pixels to Pictures — students build a picture using colored squares on graph paper (like pixels).
  • History: History of Information Transfer — students research and compare telegraph, telephone, radio, and internet.
  • Hands-On: Bits and Bytes — students encode letters using binary (1s and 0s).
  • Engineer It: Communication Solution — students design a code to send a secret message across the classroom.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Digital devices convert sound, text, and pictures into patterns of 1s and 0s (binary).
  • These patterns travel as electrical or light signals through wires, cables, or airwaves.
  • Modern devices — phones, computers, satellites — can send huge amounts of information around the world quickly.
  • Older systems like Morse code and telegraph also used patterns to transfer information.
