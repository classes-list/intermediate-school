🌍 Earth's Features and Resources

Essential Question: Where are Earth's features, hazards, and resources located, and how do we use them wisely?

📖 Unit Storyline

Students use maps to identify patterns in where natural hazards occur, explore ways to reduce their impact, and learn how technology is changing how we use Earth's natural resources.

LESSON 1 Patterns on Earth
NGSS: 4-ESS2-2

🎯 Objective

Analyze and interpret data from maps to describe patterns of Earth's features.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Maps of earthquakes and volcanoes show they appear along the same lines all over the world.

🧪 Activities

  • Hands-On: Tracking Quakes — students plot real earthquake data on a world map.
  • Discussion: Volcanic Eruptions — students compare the Pacific "Ring of Fire" to earthquake maps.
  • Investigation: By Land or By Sea — students look at ocean-floor maps and identify mid-ocean ridges.
  • Analysis: Finding Patterns — students describe how mountain ranges follow plate boundaries.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Earthquakes and volcanoes cluster along the boundaries of Earth's tectonic plates.
  • The Ring of Fire surrounds the Pacific Ocean and is a hotspot for volcanoes and earthquakes.
  • Topographic maps show elevation using contour lines — mountains, valleys, and plains.
  • By looking at patterns on maps, scientists predict where hazards are likely.

LESSON 2 Natural Hazards
NGSS: 4-ESS3-2, 3-5-ETS1-1

🎯 Objective

Generate and compare multiple solutions to reduce the impact of natural Earth processes on humans.

Anchoring Phenomenon: After a hurricane, some houses stand strong while others are destroyed. Why?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Structures — students test how different building shapes withstand wind (a fan).
  • Hands-On: Make Your Own Seismograph — students build a simple seismograph from paper and a marker.
  • Discussion: Natural Hazards — students compare hurricanes, tornadoes, wildfires, floods, and earthquakes.
  • Engineering Challenge: Natural Hazard Solutions — students design a solution to protect a town from one hazard.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Natural hazards include earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, hurricanes, tornadoes, floods, tsunamis, and wildfires.
  • We can't stop natural hazards but engineering can reduce their impact.
  • Examples: earthquake-safe buildings, flood levees, hurricane shutters, tsunami warning systems.
  • Preparation and early warning save lives.

LESSON 3 Earth's Resources
NGSS: 4-ESS3-1

🎯 Objective

Obtain and combine information to describe that energy and fuels are derived from natural resources, and their uses affect the environment.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Solar panels and wind turbines are appearing on more roofs and hillsides. Why now?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Modeling Energy Resource Use — students track energy sources used in a day.
  • Engineering: Running on Sunshine — students build a mini solar oven.
  • Hands-On: Materials We Use — students categorize items around them by resource type.
  • Investigation: Exploring Renewable Resources — students research one renewable resource (solar, wind, geothermal, hydro).
  • Debate: Pros and Cons — students compare renewable vs. nonrenewable resources.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Natural resources come from Earth: water, soil, minerals, forests, sunlight, wind.
  • Renewable resources (sun, wind, water, wood) can be replaced within a human lifetime.
  • Nonrenewable resources (coal, oil, natural gas) take millions of years to form.
  • Using resources affects the environment; sustainable practices help protect it for the future.

LESSON 4 Nonrenewable Resources & Impact
NGSS: 4-ESS3-1

🎯 Objective

Investigate how nonrenewable resources form, how humans use them, and how their use affects the environment.

Anchoring Phenomenon: Every gallon of gasoline in a car was once ancient plant and animal matter buried millions of years ago.

🧪 Activities

  • Time-Line Activity: Fossils to Fuel — students trace how coal, oil, and natural gas took millions of years to form.
  • Map Study: Where in the World? — students look at a world map of oil, coal, and gas locations and discuss why they cluster.
  • Debate: Ban the Straw? — students explore the plastic-from-oil connection and pros/cons of single-use plastic.
  • Compare Chart — students calculate how many years of coal use it takes to consume 1 year of formation.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Coal, oil, and natural gas are called fossil fuels because they formed from ancient life.
  • Fossil fuels take millions of years to form but are used up in days.
  • Burning them releases carbon dioxide, which affects Earth's climate.
  • Metals and minerals are also nonrenewable and must be mined.

LESSON 5 Sustainability & Protecting Resources
NGSS: 4-ESS3-1, 3-5-ETS1-1

🎯 Objective

Explain and design solutions that help conserve Earth's resources for future generations.

Anchoring Phenomenon: One classroom's daily plastic waste, over 10 years, could fill a room. What can we do differently?

🧪 Activities

  • Investigation: Trash Audit — students weigh classroom trash for one day and classify it (compost / recycle / trash).
  • Engineering: Design a "Reduce Station" — students design a reusable-water-bottle station for the classroom.
  • Field Trip / Video: How things get recycled at a local plant.
  • Class Pledge — students write and post a class commitment to reduce, reuse, recycle.

💡 Key Ideas

  • Sustainability means using resources in a way that leaves plenty for the future.
  • The 3 Rs: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.
  • Small daily actions (turn off water, walk instead of ride, use a reusable bottle) add up.
  • Mālama ʻĀina — caring for the land — is a Hawaiian value that fits with sustainability.
